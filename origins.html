Origins

Paris, 2003

chaleur began with an experience I lived from inside one of the populations these events harm most. In August 2003, seven years into living in Paris, I was heavily pregnant with my second child (I gave birth in the final days of the heatwave) and caring for a toddler when the heat that killed roughly 15,000 people across France settled over the city. Pregnant women and young children sit alongside the elderly among the groups public health authorities recognise as most at risk in extreme heat (French Ministry of Health, Service-Public)1, and enduring those weeks in an apartment without air conditioning, first heavily pregnant and then with a newborn and a toddler, gave me an embodied understanding of how quickly environmental heat becomes dangerous for anyone who cannot easily escape it.

What protected me was less resilience than attention. As a new mother I was surrounded by focused care, with family and friends visiting constantly, fussing over the baby, noticing if I looked unwell and ensuring I was never alone for long, and that density of attention made me far less vulnerable than my circumstances alone would suggest. The people who died in Paris that August had the opposite experience, and that contrast turned out to be the whole point. I carried the recognition with me when I moved to Sydney the following August, in 2004: that the decisive factor in who comes through such an event is rarely the hazard itself and almost always the strength of the connections around the person exposed to it.

Isolation, and France's answer

The work that gave that recognition an evidentiary spine was Richard Keller's Fatal Isolation: The Devastating Paris Heat Wave of 2003 (Keller, 2015)2, which built in turn on Eric Klinenberg's social autopsy of the 1995 Chicago heatwave and its finding that social isolation, more than heat, determined who died.3 Keller is precise about mechanism: the elderly died in such numbers not simply because their bodies were vulnerable to heat, but because their social networks had frayed, leaving them isolated and unable to reach the help they needed, often in small top-floor apartments whose zinc roofs turned the rooms into ovens. His warning is the lesson most relevant to anyone designing a response, namely that risk models built around the elderly are blunt instruments that obscure the role of poverty and isolation and overlook the thousands of other victims an age-based profile never captures.

France's institutional answer, the 2004 plan canicule and the municipal register of vulnerable residents (established by the law of 30 June 2004 and the decree of 1 September 2004)4, was a serious and humane intervention, yet it carries two limitations chaleur was designed around. The first is that it depends on a person being recognised as vulnerable and formally enrolled, whether by themselves or by a third party, which systematically misses the large group of people who manage independently, do not see themselves as at risk, and would never place their own name on a council list. The second is that it achieves coverage by assembling a centralised, name-identified register of health-vulnerable citizens, a privacy posture significant enough that France's own data-protection authority, the CNIL, had to insist these registers not become "population files".5

chaleur inverts that logic. Rather than asking the vulnerable person to identify themselves and enrol, it equips the person who already worries about them, the adult child, the neighbour or the friend, to register them, receive the warning and act, while the vulnerable person need do nothing and need never adopt the label of vulnerability. Because the sensitive information stays with that carer rather than in a government database, the model is designed to deliver the protective effect of a vulnerability register without ever constructing the centralised file of health data such registers necessarily create, which is a deliberate design choice rather than an incidental one.

Two paths lead into chaleur. A person can set it up for themselves, holding their own profile and consenting for themselves, which gives a vulnerable person agency rather than a label. More often a carer sets it up for someone they look after, and where that person can consent, the carer is asked to confirm the relationship and to record that the person has agreed to be added. The harder case is real and chaleur does not wave it away: where a person cannot consent, or does not know a profile exists, the design's answer is to hold that information only on the carer's own device, never in a central register, and to treat it as sensitive throughout. Holding the data carefully is not the same as resolving the question of consent and autonomy, and a formal pilot will need a consent and ethics framework that a clinical and ethics partner would help design.

Sydney, and the Black Summer

Australia did not leave the problem behind; years after that move, I lived through a structurally identical event under a different hazard. Through the 2019–20 Black Summer in Sydney, bushfire smoke blanketed the city I now lived in and much of eastern Australia for weeks, with population-weighted PM2.5 peaking at 98.5 µg/m³ on 14 January 2020, four times the national 24-hour standard and fourteen times the historical average, and a preliminary evaluation in the Medical Journal of Australia attributed to that smoke, across the four eastern states alone, 417 excess deaths, 1,124 cardiovascular hospitalisations, 2,027 respiratory hospitalisations and 1,305 emergency presentations for asthma (Borchers Arriagada et al., 2020)6. The pattern Keller had described in Paris held in my own city, in that the hazard was environmental, the harm fell on people with cardiac and respiratory conditions, and the people best placed to intervene were those who already cared about them.

The event that bears most directly on how chaleur is built is one I did not live through but have studied closely, and it predates that smoke by three years. On the evening of 21 November 2016, an unusual combination of extreme grass-pollen concentrations and a particular storm front produced over Melbourne, within thirty hours, 3,365 excess respiratory presentations to emergency departments and 476 excess asthma admissions, overwhelmed emergency services to the point that disaster codes were activated, and killed ten people (Thien et al., 2018)7. Only 28% of those who presented had current doctor-diagnosed asthma, which is to say that most were people an opt-in vulnerability register could never have identified in advance, yet many of whom had someone who would have acted on a warning had one reached them. That is the lesson heat and smoke only imply and the thunderstorm makes explicit, namely that the people most exposed are frequently those who never counted themselves at risk, so any response that waits for them to come forward will fail to reach them.

Why chaleur takes this form

“isolation decides who is reached in time and connection decides who survives”

These events differ in kind and in cause, an anticyclonic heat dome over Western Europe, smoke from a fire season driven by prolonged drought and a record-strength Indian Ocean Dipole, and a rare coincidence of pollen and storm over Melbourne, and that difference is precisely the point. The hazards share no single climate driver; what they share is the social mechanism beneath the deaths, in which isolation decides who is reached in time and connection decides who survives. That is the through-line from Paris in 2003, through Melbourne in 2016, to the Black Summer of 2019–20, and it is why chaleur takes the form it does, being built to activate and strengthen the local relationships that actually determine survival and to turn a forecast into a coordinated act of attention by the people already looking out for someone they love.

The case for having it operational is only sharpening. Each Australian summer now forms against a warming baseline, since the ocean and air temperatures these events build on have been climbing for decades, so whether or not any single season brings a strong El Niño, the direction of risk is set. The Bureau of Meteorology's outlook for the coming summer points to elevated heat risk8, and while the Bureau is appropriately cautious that an early signal does not guarantee strong impacts on Australia, the underlying trend means the argument for having chaleur ready before the next severe season is stronger than at any earlier point in the project.

The founder

About Natasha

Seeing what determines survival in these events is one thing; turning that into a product a clinical partner could put in front of vulnerable people is another. That is the part I do. I lead chaleur's product direction, own its privacy and security architecture, and built the current working prototype myself as a solo, pre-funded effort.

That product is built around its privacy architecture rather than having one retrofitted to it. The design keeps all sensitive health and contact information on the carer's own device under encryption, with nothing held on chaleur's servers, and only a thin, encrypted coordination signal (the bare fact that one co-carer has already acted) ever passes between carers, relayed through a server that cannot read it. The model is therefore designed to deliver a vulnerability register's protective effect without ever assembling the centralised file of health data that such registers require, and the working prototype is built toward that architecture, so the privacy model is a foundational decision rather than a feature to be added later.

Holding the product and its security in the same hands is deliberate, and both are work I have done before. At a multi-jurisdictional software company I was Head of Business Analysis, defining what the software needed to do and shaping it from what its users and stakeholders required, before serving as its chief information security officer, where I designed and operated an enterprise information security management system independently audited against ISO 27001 and SOC 2. My privacy work spans the Privacy Act 1988, the GDPR and HIPAA (including advising on HIPAA requirements in a healthcare context), and it rests on a legal foundation of a BA/LLB with First Class Honours from the University of Sydney together with postgraduate degrees in international business law and European Union law.

However, a product like this needs two kinds of expertise. The clinical judgement (which conditions and thresholds should raise an alert, and how to speak to a carer about someone's health) belongs with clinicians, and chaleur is built to take those decisions from clinical partners rather than to invent them. The product and engineering leadership belongs with me: defining what must be built, directing the team that builds it, and holding privacy and security sound by design throughout. Grant funding would resource that engineering effort and commission the independent security, cryptography and clinical review the product must pass before it is placed in front of a real, vulnerable person. I am currently seeking a clinical partner to shape its clinical content and to act as co-applicant for the funding that would take it to a council- or state-level rollout.
