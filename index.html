Community climate resilience

Turning hazard warnings into mobilised communities.

chaleur is a mobile application, currently in development, that helps you look after someone (or manage your own risk) when the climate turns dangerous. It watches the official warnings for heat, air quality, UV and thunderstorm asthma, and when conditions reach a point that could be dangerous for people with specific health profiles, it surfaces guidance on what to do and prompts action (a call, a message, a reminder, or a check-in from a community contact) so people act in time. Whether prompting action this way improves health outcomes is the question chaleur is being built to help clinical partners study.

Why chaleur exists

Send the warning to the person who already worries.

Extreme heat kills the isolated first. Studies of who dies in heatwaves have repeatedly found that living alone and lacking social contact are among the strongest risk factors, and that contact during dangerous conditions is one of the few protective factors a community can influence; the people who died when the August 2003 heatwave killed roughly 15,000 across France were disproportionately those who were alone.

The established responses both miss the people they most need to reach. Warnings broadcast to everyone fail at a documented point, because the people most at risk frequently do not count themselves among them, so the warning is addressed to exactly the population least likely to act on it. Opt-in vulnerability registers (France's institutional answer after 2003) reach only those willing to enrol under the label of vulnerability, and they achieve their coverage by assembling a centralised, name-identified file of health data.

chaleur starts from the other end of the relationship. It addresses the warning to the person who already worries (an adult child, a neighbour, a friend), someone who needs no persuading that the risk is real and is close enough to act on it. Because that carer holds the details on their own device, the design delivers the protective effect of a vulnerability register without ever constructing the centralised file of health data such registers require.

Routing the warning through a carer raises an obvious question about the vulnerable person's agency, and chaleur is designed around that question rather than past it. A person can set chaleur up for themselves, holding their own profile and consenting for themselves; where a carer sets it up instead for someone able to consent, the design asks the carer to confirm the relationship and to record that agreement. The self-service path exists precisely to preserve agency; the evidence above is why the design cannot rely on it as the only path in. The harder cases, and the consent and ethics framework a pilot would need, are set out openly on the Privacy page.

What it does

Four hazards, one prompt to the person already paying attention.

chaleur reads conditions from official sources and translates them into a clear, timely prompt, leaving every judgement about a particular person to the person who knows them.

Extreme heat

Tracks official heat warnings and flags the days when staying cool stops being routine and starts being urgent.

Poor air quality

Watches smoke and air-quality categories from state environment agencies, named exactly as the source reports them.

Extreme UV

Surfaces the sun-protection window when ultraviolet levels reach the point at which official advice says to act.

Thunderstorm asthma

Relays epidemic thunderstorm-asthma warnings in the places and seasons where authorities issue them.

chaleur reads the climate, not the person. It can tell when the conditions around someone you care about have turned dangerous, and it says so plainly; what that person actually needs, and whether to act, stays the carer's judgement.

Project status

Account setup and on-device profiles, hazard alerts with plain-language guidance, a community circle, and co-carer coordination are all built and working in the prototype today.

Not claimed yet

  • —chaleur is not publicly available to download
  • —Nothing in it is clinically validated; its guidance is provisional and drawn from official public-health sources, pending expert sign-off
  • —It carries no clinical, council or government endorsement
  • —Secure cross-device sync for co-carer coordination is designed and pending independent review; it is not yet built
  • —Clinical, security and privacy reviews are planned gates before any real-world use

chaleur is a prompt, not a safety net. An alert depends on a phone that is on, in range and able to receive it, and on a forecast that can be wrong or arrive late, so it can miss. It is built to support a carer's own attention, never to replace it, and it is not a substitute for medical advice or emergency services. If someone is unwell, contact a doctor or call 000.
