Isolation is among the deadliest risk factors in a heatwave, and one of the few you can change.

That extreme heat, bushfire smoke and thunderstorm asthma raise illness and death, and that isolation decides much of who is harmed, is established science. Read more about how that pattern recurred across Paris in 2003, the Black Summer of 2019-20 and the 2016 Melbourne thunderstorm asthma event.

Social connection as a protective factor

The risk chaleur is built around is well evidenced. Social isolation and living alone carry an increase in mortality risk comparable in magnitude to long-recognised factors such as smoking, physical inactivity and obesity (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2010; 2015), and that association holds after adjustment for prior health, which weakens the objection that it merely reflects sick people withdrawing from contact. In the specific setting of extreme heat, studies of who dies have repeatedly found that people who are isolated or rarely leave home are among those most at risk, and that social contact during dangerous conditions is one of the few protective factors a community can influence (Semenza et al., 1996; Bouchama et al., 2007). The premise that connection protects, and that isolation is a modifiable part of the risk, is therefore not in serious dispute.

Limits

What is not yet settled is whether acting on that risk in the way chaleur proposes actually helps. The protective effect of social contact in heatwaves is drawn almost entirely from observational studies, which establish an association rather than test an intervention, and chaleur's approach is a design hypothesis rather than a finding, because no comparable tool has been trialled in this form. These are the questions a clinical partner and a pilot would help answer — see the Partners page.
